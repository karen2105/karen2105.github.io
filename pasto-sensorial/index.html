Blacks' and Whites' Carnival

On the 30th September of 2009, UNESCO declared the Blacks’ and Whites’ Carnival as one of the centerpieces of the Intangible Heritage of Humanity. This massive important carnival takes place in San Juan de Pasto, which is also called "The Surprise City", in the south-west of one of the most beautiful countries, Colombia. It is performed from the 28th December to the 6th January each year and invites all travellers around the world to its Blacks’ and Whites’ cultural experience.

"We must learn to enjoy the happy moments, which are scarce during life, to enjoy them with delight, with pleasure, with elegance, and with vanity"

Carlos Albornoz - Nikito

The Blacks’ and Whites’ Carnival was born as a result of the convergence between Colonial Spaniard Culture, PreColombian Indigenous Culture and Black Culture from Africa. It involves the expression of several cultures from the Andina Zone, Amazonia Zone and Pacific Zone.

Main Days of the Blacks' and Whites' Carnival

Rainbow on the asphalt

On the 28th December, Pasto celebrates the Blacks' and Whites' opening event called “Rainbow on the Asphalt”. Its main aim is to exhibit creativity and positive messages through art, imagination and chalk. The colorful chalks used year after year to paint the grey streets gather more than 10 thousand people at Colorado Street close to Santiago Church. In that way the people generate a festive atmosphere, which everybody is invited to join in with and enjoy the Carnival.


The little carnival

This little carnival started as a child's game in which the idea was to imitate ‘Whites' Day’. Nowadays, this is celebrated on the 2nd January and it pays tribute to the Andean sentiment, to the Pachamama, to the ancestral memory and to its cosmogony.

Its aim is to develop feelings of belonging and creative skills in the younger ones, who with time will end up participating in the Magnum event on the 6th January.

Arrival of the Castañeda family

On the 4th January 1928, the Castañeda family arrived in Pasto. They were a group of people who returned from a failed colonization project in Putumayo, their original journey was towards Las Lajas Sanctuary but they ended up lost in Pasto the day before the celebration of the Blacks’ Day. Torres Arellana was the one who started shouting ¡Que viva la familia Castañeda! And it was funny enough to start imitating them.

This family represents traditional moments of the ancient Pasto people and their costumes. The main characters are: The extravagant grandmother, the pregnant daughter, cheeky children, parents, babysitters, and a drunk priest, among others.

Blacks' day

The history of the Carnival goes back to 1607 when the black slaves rose up against their masters in Remedios – Antioquia and caused panic among the Spanish authorities. This gave a precedent, years after, to black slaves in Popayan – Cauca, who realised they could claim a Freedom Day from their owners as well. This claim was positively endorsed by the King of Spain on the 5th January, and it was declared as the Freedom Day for black people.

In the early XIX century, colony authorities prohibited the festivities in order to avoid indigenous uprisings. But in 1834, the tradition started again, and the first carnival expressions were seen, leading to what it is today the Carnival. Nowadays, this Carnival remembers and celebrates the ‘Blacks' Day’. On this day people paint themselves and each other black, because they want to give a message of equality, and also want to homogenize all social classes and ethnic groups, as one large family.

Whites' day

"Whites’ Day" was born in 1912 on the morning of 6th January. The story says that Ángel María López, who was with a group of friends in a bar located in Pasto, took from a lady her powder face make-up and started to paint one of his friends, then he shouted cheers ¡Que viva el negrito y que viva el blanquito!, immediately his friend took the powder face makeup and painted other people in the bar. After that event, they went out and walked towards the Central Square -nowadays called the Nariño Square- painting all passers-by and shouting ¡Que vivan los blancos!
